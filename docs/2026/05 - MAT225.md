**Course Description:**
This course provides a rigorous introduction to single-variable calculus, focusing on the study of change through limits, continuity, differentiation, and integration. The curriculum emphasizes the conceptual understanding of algebraic, trigonometric, exponential, and logarithmic functions and their applications in science and engineering.

---

## Key Topics & Concepts

This course establishes the mathematical framework for analyzing dynamic systems and optimization:

* **Limits & Continuity:** Applying laws of limits to analyze function behavior at specific points and at infinity.
* **Differentiation:** Mastering techniques including the Product, Quotient, and Chain Rules, as well as implicit differentiation and higher-order derivatives.
* **Applications of Derivatives:** Using L’Hôpital’s Rule for indeterminate forms and applying optimization and related rates to real-world scenarios.
* **Integration:** Calculating antiderivatives and definite integrals, culminating in the application of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.
* **Foundational Competencies:**
  * **MAT-10282:** Apply the rules and laws of limits to functions.
  * **MAT-20283:** Evaluate a function by applying the techniques of differentiation.
  * **MAT-20284:** Calculate antiderivatives of a function using integration techniques.

## Major Projects & Assignments

The following projects were completed within a simulated "game tester" environment, where calculus problems served as "passwords" and "navigation data" for a sci-fi survival scenario.

1. **Project One: The Abandoned City (Limits and Differentiation)**
    * **Focus:** Mastering limits (one-sided, infinite, and at infinity) and the core rules of differentiation.
    * **Application:** Solved 12 complex problems involving Chain Rule applications for polynomial, radical, and trigonometric functions to "unlock gates" in a game world. This required providing detailed process explanations for the game developers to verify question effectiveness.

2. **Project Two: The Escape Plan (Applications and Integration)**
    * **Focus:** Applying derivatives to physics-based movement and introducing integral calculus.
    * **Application:** Analyzed position, velocity, and acceleration to locate a spaceship and calculate fuel requirements through chemical reaction rates. Tasks included optimization, linear approximation, and calculating definite/indefinite integrals.

3. **Discussion Activity: Continuity and Differentiability Analysis**
    * **Focus:** This activity required a two-stage verification of a piecewise function at a specific transition point (x = 8) to prove that a function can be continuous while also being differentiable.
    * **Methodology:**
        * **Stage 1: Continuity Verification:** Applied the formal three-part definition of continuity by evaluating left-hand limits, right-hand limits, and the defined function value to confirm the graph had no holes or jumps at the transition point.
        * **Stage 2: Differentiability Verification:** Analyzed the "smoothness" of the transition by comparing one-sided derivatives using the Chain Rule. Proved the slopes from both directions were identical (1/2), confirming the function lacked a "corner".

4. **Discussion Activity: Finding a Function to Match a Shape**
    * **Focus:** Generating a function f(x) by reverse-engineering specific target properties: decreasing at x = -6, a local minimum at x = -2, and a local maximum at x = 2.
    * **Methodology:**
        * **Derivative Construction:** Started by constructing a derivative f'(x) with roots at -2 and 2, resulting in the quadratic form f'(x) = a(x^2 - 4).
        * **Constant Determination:** Evaluated f'(-6) to ensure the function was decreasing (negative slope) at that point, selecting a = -1 to satisfy the requirement.
        * **Integration:** Applied the power rule for integration to f'(x) = -x^2 + 4 to derive the original function, resulting in f(x) = 4x - (x^3 / 3).
    * **Engineering Parallel:** Analyzed how finding a function to match a specific shape is used in design and signal processing, such as noise reduction in engineering.

## Personal Reflection & Takeaways

The most valuable takeaway from this course was mastering derivatives and understanding their role as the primary tool for measuring instantaneous change. Realizing that a derivative is not just a symbolic operation but a functional representation of a rate of change—such as the transition from position to velocity—provided a vital analytical lens for software engineering. This mathematical rigor is essential for optimizing algorithms and understanding the performance limits of the systems I build.

Conversely, the most significant cognitive challenge was wrapping my brain around the concept of integration as the inverse operation of differentiation. While the mechanics of the power rule for integration were straightforward, visualizing how calculating the area under a curve conceptually "reverses" the process of finding a tangent slope required persistent effort. The eventual "aha" moment came through the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, which bridged these two pillars of mathematics into a cohesive system.

This course directly impacts my career path by providing the foundational engineering prerequisites necessary for a Computer Science degree. I want to cultivate a rigorous mathematical background similar to that of Donald Knuth and Leslie Lamport to elevate my capabilities as a computer scientist. By mastering the "math under the hood," I am better equipped to move beyond basic syntax and contribute to the theoretical advancements of the field.
