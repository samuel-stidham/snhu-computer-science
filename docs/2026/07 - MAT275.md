**Course Description:**
This course deepens the study of single-variable calculus through advanced integration techniques and the study of infinite series. Students explore how to represent functions as polynomials with infinitely many terms and use parametric curves and polar coordinates to describe the motion of physical objects.

---

## Key Topics & Concepts

This course focuses on extending integration skills and introducing the mathematical framework for infinite summations:

* **Advanced Integration:** Mastering new techniques including substitution, integration by parts, partial fractions, and the use of integration tables.
* **Infinite Series:** Extending addition to infinitely many numbers to represent differentiable functions (exponential, trigonometric, and logarithmic) as power series.
* **Convergence Testing:** Determining if a series converges using various methods:
  * Integral Test
  * Comparison Test
  * Ratio and Root Tests
  * Alternating Series and Absolute Convergence Tests
* **Taylor & Maclaurin Series:** Generating series from infinitely differentiable functions and determining their radius of convergence.
* **Parametric Curves:** Parameterizing curves to determine slopes, lengths, and areas associated with moving objects.
* **Polar Coordinates:** Graphing equations and calculating area and arc length in polar coordinate systems.
* **Differential Equations:** Solving first-order ODEs via separation of variables, Euler's method, and applying Newton's Law of Cooling.
* **Vectors:** Computing with vectors in two and three dimensions, including unit vectors, component decomposition, and equations of planes and spheres.

## Major Projects & Assignments

The following projects were completed in a simulated internship at "Taylor-Maclaurin Solutions" to assess mathematical and problem-solving capabilities.

1. **Project One: Internship Skills Inventory Round One (100%)**
    * **Focus:** Advanced integration techniques, applications of integration, and convergence tests for sequences and series.
    * **Application:** Solved 10 inventory questions covering expected value via improper integration, mass of a lamina, area between curves, arc length, sum of series, and convergence analysis using the Integral Test, Comparison Test, Root Test, and Alternating Series Test.
    * **Methodology:** Each solution followed a structured expository format—Strategy, Solution, Final Answer, and Notes—with every technique named, justified, and verified. For example, the expected lifetime problem required splitting an improper integral via the additive interval property, applying integration by parts, and resolving an indeterminate form with L'Hôpital's Rule.

2. **Project Two: Internship Skills Inventory Round Two (100%)**
    * **Focus:** Power series, Taylor/Maclaurin series, binomial approximation, and non-Cartesian coordinate systems.
    * **Application:** Completed 8 assessment questions: radius of convergence via the Ratio Test, Taylor series by repeated differentiation of the geometric series, Maclaurin series via logarithmic properties, binomial approximation to estimate π/2, parametric tangent lines and second derivatives, cycloid arc length and area, polar curve identification and arc length, and area of common interior of polar curves.
    * **Methodology:** Solutions maintained the same structured format with particular emphasis on citing OpenStax theorems (e.g., Theorem 6.5 for term-by-term differentiation) and providing independent verification of results. The half-angle identities proved to be indispensable tools across multiple polar and parametric arc length computations.

3. **Discussion Activities: Collaborative Problem Solving**
    * **Focus:** Peer-to-peer technical walkthroughs of weekly problem set techniques.
    * **Activities:**
        * **Module 1 — Area Between Curves:** Demonstrated how to decompose a multi-region area problem (sin x vs. cos x) by identifying intersection points, determining which curve is "on top" in each sub-region, and applying the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus to each piece.
        * **Module 3 — Limit Comparison Test:** Walked through why the LCT is preferred over the Direct Comparison Test when the inequality direction is inconclusive, using the series ∑ 1/(9ⁿ − 3ⁿ) compared against a convergent geometric series.

        * **Module 7 — Radius of Convergence:** Derived R = 27/64 for a factorial-heavy power series using the Ratio Test, with each simplification step (powers of 2, factorial telescoping, leading-term analysis) presented as its own subsection.

## Personal Reflection & Takeaways

The most challenging aspect of this course was developing intuition for infinite series convergence. Finite addition is concrete—you can verify a sum by hand. But determining whether adding infinitely many terms produces a finite number requires a fundamentally different mode of reasoning: bounding behavior at infinity rather than computing a result directly. Learning to select the right convergence test for a given series (Ratio Test for factorials, Limit Comparison for asymptotic dominance, Integral Test for monotone decreasing terms) was a process of building pattern recognition through repetition.

The most valuable skill gained was the ability to represent complex functions as power series. Taylor and Maclaurin expansions transform transcendental functions into polynomial approximations of arbitrary precision—a technique that underpins numerical computing, signal processing, and algorithm design. Understanding *why* these approximations work (term-by-term differentiation within the radius of convergence, not just memorizing formulas) provides a deeper foundation for the computational methods I will encounter in computer science.

The parametric and polar coordinate modules also revealed an important lesson: the same curve can have radically different representations depending on the coordinate system, and choosing the right one can simplify a problem from intractable to elegant. The half-angle identities transforming √(2 − 2cos θ) into a clean integrand was a concrete example of how the right algebraic insight unlocks an entire solution.

This course completes the calculus sequence required for both the Computer Science degree and the Mathematics minor. Mastery of series, integration techniques, and coordinate transformations provides the mathematical vocabulary for advanced algorithm analysis, numerical methods, and the theoretical foundations championed by Donald Knuth and Leslie Lamport—the standard I continue to hold my work to.
